This is my Favorite Topic of All Time

and my favorite thing of all time in general

If you know me at all, or even if you don't know me and just follow me on Instagram, you know that food and more specifically, pictures of food, are a top priority in my book. At some point, you stop caring about other people watching you take pictures of your food and go for the snap anyways. After that point, when almost all dignity is lost, you get to the point where you go into restaurants with a purpose and with your heavy duty camera. Is it unnecessary? Yes. Is it overkill? Yes. But will you thank yourself later? Absolutely yes. In the long run, it is actually a beneficial step because if you're ever out somewhere you have been to before, and a person you're eating with is wondering if a certain item on the menu is good, all you have to do is whip out your phone or camera, show them the picture, and ultimately save the day. So to answer the question no one asked, no, I do not regret this camera ritual. Anywho, this is the place where I'll display all the goodies and not-so-goodies that I've tasted along with a few tidbits about them. Just think of it as a food diary.

Causa Rellena

A tuna and potato casserole.

This country never fails to amaze me with its odd flavor combinations that somehow work? I don't understand? When I tried this dish for the first time, I had no idea it was called Causa Rellena; all I knew was that I saw layers of tuna and potato stacked on top of each other with olives and other flourishings on top. The closest meal to mashed potatoes you will find, the potato layer nicely balances the crunch from the tuna salad adjacent to it. However, the casserole is a staple only served on rarer occassions unlike bread that is consumed approximately eight times a day.

Cuy

Our furry friends are no longer furry and no longer our friends.

The poor little ones didn’t stand a chance. Rather than try to make this feel like you are not eating a guinea pig, cuy cooks strive to make the experience as realistic and gruesome as possible. That means that teeth, nails, eyes, internal organs, and any other body part are all fair game. Let’s just say no one owns a guinea pig as a pet in Peru. I will say though, having consumed one of these guys, the skin is the best part because the way it was cooked hardened the skin to a potato chip-like texture and consistency, and I cannot reject anything that resembles a potato chip.

Lomo Soltado

because it sounds better than its literal translation, the "jumping loin"

This dish comes with a bit of background that is quite interesting. Now you may have heard of burgers and fries, maybe even fish and fries, but what about steak and fries? In a nutshell, lomo soltado is a combo of steak and french fries, but it usually also includes rice and onions as sides. It is a hybrid of sorts, fusing Chinese with Peruvian cuisine in the early 1900s and serving as the Peruvian version of stir fry. If you like steak, and if you like french fries, there is really nothing that can go wrong here.

Inca Kola

Like Coca Cola, except Peruvian and yellow

Look to your left. Look to your right. Look in the fridge. Look in the trash. Just have the general ability to see. And Inca Kola will be there. The Peruvian equivalent of Coca Cola, Inka Kola is a drink that is difficult to describe. It’s yellow. It’s 10x sweeter than Coke. It’s bubbly. It has the faint aftertaste of bubblegum. I would say that it resembles a combination of cream soda and Sprite, but physically tasting it is the only way to do it a justice.

Empanadas

But not the empanadas you're thinking of.

Oh no no...these are the party empanadas. a weird fusion between a pop tart, a shortbread cookie, and anything but a traditional empanada. I was very confused by this cute, sprinkled pastry at first, but I fell for it hard after a while. My American self was initially expecting some jelly or jam or something to ooze out of the middle (#raisedbypoptarts), but it is quite literally just a cookie with sprinkles on it. A.k.a. more bread a.k.a. loving it. Also, my amazingly awesome host mom packed me an entire box of these bad boys on the way back home, so they had a chance to meet their American counterpart.

Choclo

Maga corn.

Think of corn, ok, but corn that goes to the gym seven days a week and is on steroids. That mental image should match this actual image you see above. Instead of biting into maybe 10 kernels, your mouth can only handle one or two at a time to munch on those suckers. One kernel is about the size of a thumb, and that is intimidating just to think about. They were super good, but I will say that I am partial to the North American corn we have in this neck of the woods. The taste is much more bland and less sweet than the corn popular up North, but it can be integrated into almost any Peruvian dish you can think of due to its mild taste that gives it its versatility.

Tres Leches

Heaven on a plate.

My goodness my goodness. This tops cheesecake; this tops red velvet cake; this tops all of my formerly beloved cakes. Why, you ask? Well, as the name denotes, there are three types of milk that are poured into holes in the cake after it is baked, and the finished product is topped with a whipped goodness that is not too rich but not at all bland either. I sampled this Peruvian staple at probably five restaurants, and not one was a disappointment. Sometimes, you can find a sliver of caramel running through the middle of the cake that elevates the cake to a new realm. Although the pastry is not as light and airy as angel food cake, the whipped topping and the moisture coming from the milk in the cake give it a run for its money and prevents any dryness over time.

Chica Morada

Imagine purple corn...in drink form.

I honestly don't know how someone someone thought of this concoction, but alas, someone did. I still have mixed feelings on it, but I will try my best to explain this strange beauty. She's purple. She's served lukewarm. She's sweet. She's a bit spicy because of the added cinnamon and cloves. Oh, and she's made from that corn you see up there. Chicha morada is sold in gobs and gobs of stores, but it tastes best when made directly from the corn and not a powder with tons of added sugars that did not really need to be added.

Torta

The most popular Peruvian cake is...unfrosted!!!

What? You mean just a cake? Yes, I mean just the plain ole cake with no bells or whistles. What does this mean? This means that the actual cake is so good and so moist that icing is not even a necessity, and you can eat this treat on any occassion and at any time of day. Basically, it is the cake that you can have when you want cake for breakfast or something, but its not a big deal. It justifies eating cake whenever you want.
